This tool tracks wildlife observation patterns and stream health along Colorado's Front Range using freely available public data — community wildlife sightings and continuous stream-flow measurements maintained by federal agencies. When streams run low, some species disappear from the corridor entirely. This dashboard surfaces those connections so conservation organizations can see where to focus attention, even without a dedicated data team.

What Happened on Plum Creek

A creek ran dry — and the birds left with it.

Starting in July 2025, Plum Creek — a narrow riparian corridor running through Highlands Ranch — spent five consecutive months carrying only 27 to 40 percent of its normal water volume. During those same months, bird activity along the creek dropped by roughly half compared to the year before. A species called the Northern Rough-winged Swallow — a small, fast-moving bird that nests in creek banks and hunts insects over open water — tracks the creek's flow cycle closely: present April through September, peaking in May when discharge is at its highest, and absent through the winter low-flow months. Independent eBird data (71 observations) confirms the pattern, with the swallow's monthly count correlating with stream discharge at r = 0.677. This is the clearest signal in the entire two-year dataset: the health of this creek and the wildlife that depends on it are directly connected, and right now, the creek is under stress.

Insects

The Insect Decline

46 high-confidence insect species are declining on the Front Range — more than any other group. Butterflies, moths, beetles, grasshoppers, and native bees all appear on the list.

Only one — Horace's Duskywing — has genuine independent corroboration: GBIF holds more records than iNaturalist for this species, confirming the decline through a truly separate data source.

Why so few confirmed? Colorado insect monitoring is almost entirely iNaturalist. GBIF aggregates iNaturalist data, so "corroboration" from GBIF often means seeing the same observations twice.

Methodology

How We Know This

Four layers of analysis, each filtering out noise from the one before.

  1. 1.iNaturalist baseline — 268,000+ research-grade observations. Observer participation up 5.2%, so species declines aren't explained by fewer people submitting data.
  2. 2.eBird cross-check — bird declines validated against Cornell Lab's dedicated birding database. 4 of 18 high-confidence bird declines confirmed.
  3. 3.RMNP comparison — declining species checked against Rocky Mountain National Park to separate urban habitat loss from regional climate signals.
  4. 4.USGS + NOAA water data — daily discharge from 4 stream gauges (Plum Creek, Big Dry Creek, South Platte) correlated with wildlife observations using observer effort normalization (taxon counts divided by total monthly regional observations) and a one-month lag structure: prior-month discharge predicts current-month observations. Results cross-validated against an Iowa agricultural watershed using identical methodology.
Water · South Platte & Plum Creek

Insects Track Streamflow on the South Platte

Over a 7-year normalized record (49 active-season months, 2019–2026), insect observations on the South Platte River corridor track discharge month-by-month. Black swallowtail — the most-observed declining insect in this dataset — shows the clearest signal: r = 0.533 against South Platte discharge (p < 0.001, n = 49). When the river runs high in spring, butterfly observations follow. This finding survived the observer-effort normalization that retracted the earlier bird–streamflow result.

At Plum Creek, the picture is more site-specific: Northern Rough-winged Swallow is a flow-correlated summer resident — present April through September, peaking in May at peak discharge (eBird: r = 0.677, 71 observations). Horace's Duskywing was absent during the seven-month low-flow period, though this coincides with the natural end of its flight season — a 7-year test found no significant relationship with discharge (r = 0.11). The insect-discharge mechanism was independently cross-validated at an Iowa agricultural watershed using the same methodology.

Next Steps · Open to All

Act On This

  1. 1. Log waterbirds along Plum Creek on iNaturalist. Every sighting here contributes to building the long-term dataset needed to track how wildlife responds to flow conditions.
  2. 2. Run a kick-net survey on Plum Creek. Caddisflies and stoneflies — the most sensitive biological indicators of stream health — show zero observations in this corridor. One physical survey in spring and late summer would confirm whether they've disappeared or just gone unsampled.
  3. 3. Log insect observations along Plum Creek, especially in spring. A corridor-level analysis found that most of the 46 declining insect species are rarely or never recorded along Plum Creek — upland and foothill species make up the bulk of regional observations. Species that do use the creek (Black swallowtail, Pale green assassin bug, and Nessus sphinx appear most consistently) are the ones worth watching for long-term trends.
  4. 4. Watch Black swallowtail at South Platte access points. This common butterfly tracks South Platte discharge over a 7-year normalized record (r = 0.53, p < 0.001, n = 49 months). It's a visible, cost-free indicator for the health of Colorado's main stem river — one any observer can contribute to.
  5. 5. Build on this data. Every script, dataset, and methodology behind this report is publicly available on GitHub. Replicate it, extend it, or adapt it to a different watershed.

Data: April 2024 – January 2026  ·  South Skunk River near Ames & Colfax, Iowa  ·  2 USGS gauges

The South Skunk River runs through Story County, Iowa — a landscape of corn and soybean fields that defines the agricultural Midwest. Using the same methodology developed on Colorado's Front Range, this analysis asks whether freely available public data can detect the health of a farm-country stream: when it's stressed, when it recovers, and where agricultural chemistry leaves a fingerprint on the wildlife community.

What the Data Shows on the South Skunk River

More water in one month. More insects the next.

Across 22 months of observations, prior-month discharge on the South Skunk River reliably predicts how many insects will be observed the following month — confirmed at both USGS gauges and surviving temperature control. In June 2024, that pattern broke sharply. Atrazine — the corn herbicide applied at spring planting — reached 1.70 µg/L, approaching the EPA acute risk threshold for aquatic invertebrates (1.8 µg/L) and more than double the chronic limit for aquatic plants (0.68 µg/L). May discharge had been the second-highest in the record; the model predicted strong insect emergence. Instead, June insect observations came in at just 43% of what was predicted — the largest gap in the entire dataset.

Key Findings

What the data shows — and what we can act on.

Water Signal

Insects Follow the Water

Prior-month discharge predicts the following month's insect observations — confirmed at both USGS gauges on the South Skunk River. After controlling for temperature, the upstream Ames gauge shows partial r = 0.497 (p = 0.026). The downstream Colfax gauge is directionally consistent.

The mechanism: high water expands aquatic larval habitat — wetted margins, standing pools, hyporheic zones. Adult insects emerge and become observable the following month. This one-month lag matches the development cycle of the midges, flies, and aquatic insects that dominate this watershed.

Cross-validation: Iowa produces the strongest discharge signal of the three sites tested (Colorado, Iowa, Maryland), consistent with its more pronounced seasonal hydrology.
June 2024 · Atrazine Episode

The Model Predicted Insects.
They Didn't Appear.

May 2024 discharge was the second-highest month on record — the model predicted strong insect emergence in June. Instead, normalized insect observations were 43% of the predicted level — the largest negative residual in the 22-month dataset.

Atrazine: 1.70 µg/L
70% above the EPA chronic aquatic life criterion (1.0 µg/L)  ·  96th percentile of all measurements at this site since 1999
2.5× the chronic plant limit
EPA chronic aquatic plant benchmark: 0.68 µg/L  ·  Atrazine suppresses the algae and submerged plants that insect larvae depend on for food
Important: Direction is unambiguous — but one month of coincidence is not causation. Each additional June with both pesticide and observation data narrows the uncertainty.
Professional Validation · Iowa DNR BioNet

Two Methods, Same Story

Iowa DNR biological assessors have sampled this reach using standardized kick-net surveys. Their BMIBI score (Biological Macroinvertebrate Index of Biotic Integrity) places it in the marginally impaired range: EPT insects are present but sparse.

Professional BMIBI: 55–66
Pass/fail threshold is 62. The Story City reach consistently falls just below it. Aquatic life: "Not Supported."
iNaturalist EPT: 36 observations
11 mayflies · 12 stoneflies · 13 caddisflies across 22 months — sparse but present, and flow-sensitive. Exactly what a BMIBI 55–66 stream produces.
What this means: Community science picked up the same ecological condition as professional sampling — from a completely different method, with no biological survey protocol.
Mechanism · Functional Groups

4% of Observations Drive the Signal

Of 3,188 insect observations, just 4% are aquatic-emergent — EPT insects (mayflies, stoneflies, caddisflies) plus Odonata (dragonflies and damselflies). The other 96% are terrestrial: butterflies, bees, beetles, grasshoppers.

EPT + Odonata discharge signal
r = 0.539  ·  p = 0.006  ·  from just 141 observations across 22 months

The correlation isn't coming from butterflies and bumblebees. It's coming from the insects with aquatic larval stages — which confirms the mechanism, not just the correlation.

Why it matters: Targeted EPT monitoring — even from community observers — may detect flow-sensitive stress signals that broad insect counts miss.

Stream context: The South Skunk River near Ames carries three concurrent stressors at mean conditions — E. coli at roughly 5× the recreational contact standard, nitrate exceeding the EPA drinking water limit, and seasonal herbicide pulses each spring at planting. The discharge signal may integrate all of these: high-flow years flush agricultural chemicals through the watershed faster; low-flow years concentrate them. Water column sampling detects herbicides like atrazine but cannot detect pyrethroid insecticides such as bifenthrin, which bind to sediment. USGS found bifenthrin in sediment at 99 of 100 Midwest agricultural streams sampled in 2013 — including sites 21km from this corridor. No single stressor explains the insect pattern alone.

Methodology · Three Locations

How It Works

The same method was tested in three different places. What it found depended on where it was.

Colorado · Front Range
Driver: Discharge
Arid west  ·  50-mile radius, Highlands Ranch

Water is the limiting resource. When rivers and creeks run lower than normal, insects and birds that depend on riparian corridors follow. Black swallowtail tracks South Platte discharge over a 7-year normalized record (r = 0.533, p < 0.001, n = 49 months). Northern Rough-winged Swallow tracks Plum Creek flow seasonally (eBird: r = 0.677).

What limits this watershed: available water in an arid climate. Flow below normal = wildlife below normal.
Iowa · South Skunk River
Driver: Discharge
Agricultural midwest  ·  Ames to Colfax, Story County

The same pattern holds in the humid midwest: prior-month discharge predicts the following month's insect observations (partial r = 0.497 after temperature control). Here, the discharge signal also integrates agricultural stress — the June 2024 atrazine episode produced the largest insect gap in the dataset precisely when the discharge model predicted abundance. The nearest USGS Midwest Stream Quality Assessment site (Walnut Creek, 21km south) found sediment-bound pyrethroid insecticides and atrazine-related diatom suppression in 2013 — suggesting the South Skunk faces a multi-stressor load that water column sampling alone cannot fully characterize.

What limits this watershed: discharge integrates agricultural stress — high-flow years dilute and flush; low-flow years concentrate.
Maryland · Patuxent River
Driver: Temperature
Humid east  ·  Laurel to Bowie, Prince George's County

A different story entirely. In the humid east, water is rarely scarce — precipitation arrives year-round and rivers stay full. Temperature is what drives insect emergence: same-month temperature correlates with insect observations at r = 0.889. Prior-month discharge adds nothing independent (partial r = 0.067, p = 0.71).

What limits this watershed: temperature. Spring warming drives emergence regardless of how much water is in the river.

The central finding across all three sites: the dominant driver of wildlife observations is not the same everywhere. In water-limited western states, scarcity sets the limit. In the humid east, temperature does. A monitoring system designed only for the arid west will produce wrong answers in Maryland. The right question isn't "does water drive wildlife?" — it's "what limits this watershed?"
